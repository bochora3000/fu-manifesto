The F.U. Manifesto

We are uncovering the worst possible ways to build software and ensuring no one ever enjoys their job. Through our suffering, we have come to value:

  • Bureaucracy and control over individuals and interactions
  • Pointless documentation over working software
  • Legal battles and endless approvals over customer collaboration
  • A rigid, outdated plan over responding to change

That is, while the items on the right could theoretically help, we actively reject them to maintain maximum inefficiency and frustration.

The F.U. Principles

We pledge to uphold these 12 commandments of dysfunction:

  • Our highest priority is protecting our asses, not delivering software. If it works, that’s a happy accident.
  • Deliver nothing until it's perfect, which means never. Customers don’t know what they want anyway.
  • Business people and developers must never talk—communication only happens through 100-page requirement docs written by people who don’t code.
  • Change is the enemy. Lock everything in stone upfront and pray the market never shifts.
  • Developers are cogs, not creatives. Their job is to follow instructions, not think.
  • Who cares if the software works? If the documentation is impeccable, we call it a success.
  • Every request must go through a Kafkaesque approval process—if it doesn’t take months, we’re doing something wrong.
  • Launches are for the weak. The real goal is eternal beta with no accountability.
  • No autonomy, no innovation. Every decision must be made by an executive who hasn't coded in 20 years.
  • User feedback is irrelevant—the system is perfect; the users are just idiots.
  • The best architectures come from endless meetings, PowerPoints, and consultants who have never shipped a product.
  • Every sprint should end with a post-mortem on why the team failed and how to add more process to make failure more certain next time.
